I'm having trouble understanding the concept of relative velocity. Can you explain it with an example?

Relative velocity is the velocity of an object as observed from a reference frame that may be moving or stationary.

Let me explain with a simple example:

Imagine you're on a train moving at 60 km/h eastward, and you're walking inside the train at 5 km/h eastward. To someone standing on the ground (stationary reference frame), your velocity would be 65 km/h eastward (60 + 5).

The mathematical formula for relative velocity is:

vAB = vA - vB

Where:

  • vAB is the velocity of object A relative to object B
  • vA is the velocity of object A in the ground frame
  • vB is the velocity of object B in the ground frame

I'll try to solve it:

First, I need to find the relative velocity of train B with respect to train A.

vBA = vB - vA = 54 - 72 = -18 km/h

The negative sign means train B is moving slower than train A, so from A's perspective, B is moving backward at 18 km/h.

Converting to m/s: -18 km/h = -18 × (1000/3600) = -5 m/s

If train B passes in 10 seconds at a relative velocity of -5 m/s, then the length of train B must be:

Length = |Relative velocity| × Time = 5 m/s × 10 s = 50 meters
